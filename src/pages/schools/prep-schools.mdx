# Prep Schools

**The bottom line:**
- A prep year bridges the gap between Canadian high school and NCAA basketball — competition, coaching, exposure, and academics in one year
- Not every player needs one, but most Canadian D1 players go through some form of prep
- The quality spectrum is enormous — from excellent programs to borderline scams
- The #1 question to ask any program: **where have your graduates gone?** If they can't name names, walk away.
- A prep year does NOT start the NCAA eligibility clock
- Budget $10,000-$40,000+ per year. Know exactly what you're getting for that money.

---

## What Is a Prep School?

"Prep" is short for "preparatory." At its core, a prep school is a **private school** that prepares students for university or college. These schools usually:

- **Charge tuition** (they're not public schools)
- Often have **smaller class sizes** and more rigorous academics
- Sometimes are **boarding schools** (students live on campus), though many are day schools
- Put a big emphasis on **getting kids into good universities and colleges**

In the basketball and US sports context, "going prep" or "prep school" means sending a kid — often in grades 9-12, sometimes for an extra "post-grad" year — to a private school with strong academics AND a serious sports program. The idea is to get better competition, more development, and more exposure to college recruiters, while still being in a school setting.

In Canada, the term is mostly borrowed from the US system. Some Canadian private schools and programs brand themselves as "prep" because they offer that same combination: more intense basketball plus an academic structure geared toward college pathways — often in the US.

The concept as it applies to this guide: **a concentrated year (or years) of elite basketball development, competition, and exposure, combined with academics, designed to bridge the gap between Canadian high school and NCAA/USports basketball.**

Prep schools have become a central part of the Canadian-to-NCAA pipeline. As of recent years, 150+ Canadians are playing D1 basketball, and the vast majority went through some form of prep program. The prep year is so standard that US recruiting databases automatically add a year to Canadian players' class designation — "Class of 2032" means "expected to start college fall 2032, after a prep year." (See the [Reclassification Guide](/schools/reclassification) for the full explanation.)


## Why Prep Schools Exist

Canadian high school basketball, in most provinces, does not provide the level of competition, coaching, or visibility that NCAA programs are looking for. This isn't a criticism — it's a structural reality:

- **Competition level:** Standard Canadian HS basketball is not comparable to US HS basketball or prep school leagues. The talent density is lower, the coaching is often part-time, and the schedule is shorter.
- **Visibility:** NCAA coaches are not flying to Canadian cities to watch regular-season high school games. They attend sneaker circuit events, prep school league games, and showcases during NCAA evaluation periods.
- **Physical readiness:** Canadian players often graduate high school at 17-18 — younger than their US counterparts. A prep year adds physical maturity.
- **Academic alignment:** Canadian high school transcripts don't always align perfectly with NCAA Eligibility Center requirements. A prep year with accredited coursework can address gaps.

Prep schools solve all four of these problems in one year.


## Is a Prep Year Necessary?

**It depends on the player.** Not every player needs a prep year, and not every player should do one.

### When a prep year makes sense

- The player has the talent to play at the next level but needs more exposure, development, or physical maturity
- The player hasn't had heavy circuit exposure during high school and needs a concentrated year of visibility
- The player's academics need strengthening for NCAA eligibility
- The player is between options and needs time to get recruited without starting the NCAA eligibility clock (prep years do NOT start the clock)

### When a prep year might NOT be necessary

- The player has been on elite circuits (EYBL, 3SSB, UAA) with significant exposure and already has committable offers
- The player has been recruited through Canada Basketball national team events
- The player is ready — physically, mentally, and academically — to jump directly to college

### Can you skip prep and go through circuits alone?

**Yes, but it's potentially less exposure.** A player with heavy circuit presence during NCAA evaluation periods can absolutely get recruited without a prep year. The prep year can add a concentrated dose of competition, coaching attention, and visibility — but only if the program is actually playing in front of D1 coaches regularly. A prep school that doesn't compete during evaluation periods or in recognized leagues may not provide more exposure than a strong circuit program. The level of competition also varies dramatically by program — not all prep schools are playing against top-tier opponents.

### The US option

Some Canadian players and families choose to attend prep school or even high school **in the United States** rather than staying in Canada. US prep programs (IMG Academy, Huntington Prep, Brewster Academy, etc.) and some US high schools with elite basketball programs offer higher competition levels and more direct visibility to college coaches. This path comes with additional costs (international tuition, visa requirements, distance from home) but can be the right move for players who are ready and whose families can support it.


## What Makes a Good Prep School

Not all prep schools are the same. The range is enormous — from world-class programs that consistently produce D1 players to programs that take your money and deliver very little. Here's what to evaluate.

### Competition & League Affiliation

**The single best indicator of a prep school's legitimacy is what league they compete in and who they play against.**

- **NPA (National Preparatory Association)** is currently the most prominent Canadian prep basketball league, operated by [North Pole Hoops](https://northpolehoops.com/npa2025/). NPA programs compete against each other in structured sessions and draw D1 coaching attendance.
- **US circuits and tournaments:** Legitimate prep schools don't just play in Canada — they travel to compete in the US during NCAA evaluation periods. This is where real exposure happens.
- **The Grind Session, OSBA, and other leagues** provide additional competitive platforms.

**Ask:** "What league do you play in? What US events do you compete in? During which NCAA evaluation periods?"

### Coaching Staff

- **Do the coaches have college coaching experience or connections?** A prep school coach's rolodex matters — they need relationships with college programs to facilitate recruiting.
- **What's their development philosophy?** Are they building players for the next level, or running a system that wins games without developing individuals?
- **Is the coaching staff full-time?** A full-time coaching staff that trains players daily is fundamentally different from a part-time arrangement.

### Academic Program

This is where many prep schools fall short — and where the stakes are highest.

- **Is the academic program accredited?** This is critical. If the credits aren't recognized by the NCAA Eligibility Center, your kid could spend a year and $20K+ and still not be academically eligible for NCAA play.
- **Is it a real school or online coursework?** Some prep schools offer rigorous in-person academics. Others put kids in front of laptops for a few hours and call it school. The difference matters — for eligibility, for college readiness, and for your kid's education if basketball doesn't work out.
- **Does the school have a track record with the NCAA Eligibility Center?** Ask if previous graduates have been cleared by the Eligibility Center without issues.

### Player Placement Track Record

**This is the most important question: where have players gone after this program?**

- **Ask for specific names and where they ended up.** A legitimate program can point to graduates playing D1, D2, NAIA, or USports. They should be able to name names.
- **How many players from each graduating class receive post-secondary opportunities?** Not just the star player — what happens to the roster?
- **What level do graduates typically reach?** D1 Power 5? Mid-major D1? D2? USports? Knowing the realistic outcome helps you set expectations.

**Red flag:** If a program can't name graduates who are playing at the next level, or if they deflect this question, that tells you everything you need to know.

### Playing Time & Development

The same playing time concerns from club basketball apply here — possibly more so, because the financial commitment is larger.

- **How many players are on the roster?** A roster of 20 with only 8 getting minutes means 12 players are paying premium tuition to sit.
- **What's the development plan for players who aren't starting?** Good programs develop their whole roster, not just the top 5.
- **Will your kid get meaningful minutes?** This is hard to guarantee, but the program should be honest about the competitive landscape on the roster.


## The Cost Reality

Prep school is expensive. Families should go in with clear eyes about what they're spending and what they're getting.

### What to expect

- **Tuition:** Ranges widely — from $10,000 to $40,000+ per year. Some elite boarding school programs (like Ridley College) can be $50,000+.
- **Room and board:** If the player is relocating (common — many top programs are in Ontario), add housing and living costs.
- **Travel:** Prep schools travel to US events and tournaments. Some travel is included in tuition; some is extra.
- **Gear:** Some programs provide gear; others don't.

### What the money should buy

For $15,000-$30,000, you should expect:
- Full-time coaching and daily training
- Competitive league play (NPA or equivalent) with US tournament exposure
- Accredited academic program recognized by the NCAA Eligibility Center
- Strength and conditioning program
- Film/video services
- Active recruiting support from coaching staff (reaching out to college programs, facilitating visits, managing communication)

### Is it worth it?

**It can be — if the program delivers.** A legitimate prep year that produces D1 or USports opportunities can be a life-changing investment. A year at a program that doesn't develop players, doesn't provide exposure, and doesn't have college connections is money burned.

The ROI calculation is simple: **does this program have a track record of placing players at the next level?** If yes, and your kid is genuinely at that level, it can be worth the investment. If no, you're paying for a year of basketball without a pathway to anywhere.


## The Fake Prep School Problem

This is the biggest risk families face in the Canadian prep school landscape, and it's getting worse as basketball grows in Canada.

### What's happening

The boom in Canadian basketball has created demand for prep school programs. That demand has attracted a wave of programs calling themselves "prep schools" that vary dramatically in quality. Some are excellent. Some are mediocre. Some are borderline scams.

### The spectrum of quality

Prep schools exist on a wide spectrum:

**At the top end:**
- Full-time coaching staff with college experience and relationships
- Accredited academic program with in-person instruction
- Compete in NPA or equivalent leagues plus US tournaments during evaluation periods
- Consistent track record of placing players in D1, D2, NAIA, or USports programs
- Comprehensive development: basketball, academics, strength & conditioning, life skills

**In the middle:**
- Decent coaching but limited college connections
- Online or hybrid academics (may or may not be recognized by NCAA Eligibility Center)
- Some competitive play but not in top leagues or US evaluation-period events
- Some graduates reach post-secondary programs, but placement rate is unclear

**At the bottom:**
- Coaching staff with no college experience or connections
- Academics are an afterthought — online coursework with minimal oversight
- Limited competition against weak opponents, no US evaluation-period exposure
- No track record of placing players at the next level
- Marketing focuses on Instagram highlights and "exposure" promises
- In some cases, international (non-athletic) students are effectively funding the basketball program through their tuition

### How to spot a problematic program

**Red flags:**
- Can't name specific graduates and where they're playing now
- Academic program is entirely online with no in-person component
- Not a member of NPA or an equivalent recognized league
- Doesn't compete in US events during NCAA evaluation periods
- Coaches have no verifiable college coaching background or college relationships
- Promises about "D1 exposure" or "guaranteed recruitment" without evidence to back it up
- No transparency about placement rates — how many players from each class received post-secondary opportunities?
- High tuition ($15,000+) without clear articulation of what the money pays for
- The program is brand new with no graduating classes yet (not necessarily a red flag on its own, but combined with other factors, proceed with caution)

### How to vet a program

1. **Ask for a list of graduates and where they went.** This is the non-negotiable. A program that can't answer this question isn't worth your money.
2. **Verify league membership.** Is the program in the NPA? Do they compete in recognized US leagues or events?
3. **Check NCAA Eligibility Center status.** Have graduates been cleared by the Eligibility Center without academic issues?
4. **Talk to families who've been through the program.** Ask the program for references — then actually call them. Ask specifically about playing time, development, recruiting support, and academic quality.
5. **Ask college coaches.** If you have any college coaching connections (through your circuit program, for example), ask if they recruit from that prep school. If college coaches don't know the program exists, that tells you something.
6. **Visit in person if possible.** Watch a practice. Meet the coaching staff. See the academic facilities. See where players live if it's a boarding situation.

<Callout type="warning" title="The stakes are high">
A kid who spends a year (and a family that spends $15,000-$30,000) at a program that doesn't deliver gets: no meaningful development, no exposure to college coaches, academic credits that may not be recognized by the NCAA Eligibility Center, a lost year that can't be recovered, and potentially a player who's now disillusioned with the process.

Do your homework before writing the check.
</Callout>


## Prep Schools vs. Circuits — Do You Need Both?

This isn't either/or for most Canadian players — it's a sequence.

### The typical Canadian pathway

1. **Middle school / early HS:** Club basketball + middle school circuits (Jr EYBL, Jr 3SSB, MADE Hoops, etc.)
2. **High school:** Club basketball + HS circuits (EYBL, EYCL, 3SSB, UAA, etc.) during spring/summer evaluation periods. School basketball during the school year.
3. **Post-grad:** Prep school year — NPA or equivalent league play + US tournament exposure during evaluation periods
4. **College:** NCAA, USports, or other post-secondary program

### The circuit-only path

Some players skip the prep year entirely and go directly from high school to college. This works when:
- The player has been on elite circuits (EYBL, 3SSB, UAA) with heavy exposure during evaluation periods
- College coaches have already evaluated the player extensively through circuit play and/or national team events
- The player is physically, mentally, and academically ready
- The player has committable offers in hand

This path is less common for Canadian players because the structural barriers (visibility, competition level, physical readiness) that prep schools address still exist for most.

### The prep-school-first path

Some players — especially those who didn't play on major circuits during high school — use the prep year as their primary exposure vehicle. The prep school's league play, US travel, and coaching connections become the recruiting pathway.


## Key Takeaways

1. **Prep schools bridge real gaps** — competition level, visibility, physical maturity, and academics. For most Canadian players pursuing NCAA basketball, a prep year is a valuable (if not essential) part of the pathway.
2. **Not all prep schools are the same.** The range from excellent to harmful is enormous. Evaluate rigorously before committing money.
3. **The single most important question: where have graduates gone?** If a program can't point to specific players playing at the next level, be very cautious.
4. **League affiliation matters.** NPA membership and US evaluation-period competition are strong signals of legitimacy.
5. **The academic program is non-negotiable.** If credits aren't recognized by the NCAA Eligibility Center, the entire year could be wasted from an eligibility standpoint.
6. **Playing time is development time.** Don't pay $20,000+ to watch your kid sit on the bench. Understand the roster situation before committing.
7. **A prep year does NOT start the NCAA eligibility clock.** This is one of its key advantages — it's a "free" year of development and exposure.
8. **You can get to NCAA without prep — but it's harder.** The circuit-only path works for elite players with heavy exposure. For everyone else, the prep year fills gaps that are hard to fill otherwise.
9. **Do your homework.** Visit the program, talk to past families, verify with college coaches. The stakes (financial and developmental) are too high for a decision based on an Instagram page.
