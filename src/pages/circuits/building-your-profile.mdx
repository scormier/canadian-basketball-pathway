# Building Your Recruiting Profile

Talent gets you on the radar. Your profile is what gets coaches to watch. This page covers the practical side of making yourself recruitable — from creating platform profiles to sending your first email to a college coach.

**The bottom line:**
- Your kid can reach out to college coaches at any time (start in grade 10-11) — there are no NCAA restrictions on prospect-initiated contact
- A short, specific, personalized email with film links beats a generic mass email every time
- Full game film against quality competition is MORE valuable than a highlight reel. Include both.
- Stats without context are meaningless — always include the competition level and number of games
- Your coach's college relationships are one of the most important recruiting channels
- College camps are an underutilized way to get evaluated directly by a target school's coaching staff

## Recruiting Platforms — Where to Be Discoverable

These platforms help college coaches find and evaluate prospects. They're not the only channel — coach relationships and circuit performance matter more — but having a presence makes it easier for coaches to research your kid when they hear the name.

### The Key Platforms

**[NCSA (Next College Student Athlete)](https://www.ncsasports.org/)** — The largest recruiting network. Create a profile with academic info, athletic stats, video, and target schools. Coaches use NCSA to search for prospects. Free profiles are available; paid tiers offer more features.

**[Prep Hoops Canada](https://prephoops.com/)** — Rankings, event coverage, and player profiles specifically covering the Canadian landscape. Strong presence in the grassroots ecosystem. Getting covered or ranked by Prep Hoops increases visibility.

**[North Pole Hoops (NPH)](https://northpolehoops.com/)** — Canadian basketball media and scouting organization. Operates the NPA (National Preparatory Association). Player profiles and coverage add discoverability.

**Team/program website** — Many circuit and prep programs maintain player profiles on their own sites. Make sure your kid's info is up to date on their program's platform.

### What to Include in a Profile

| Element | What to Include |
|---|---|
| **Basics** | Full name, height, weight, position, graduation year (both CAN and US class), high school, club/program |
| **Academics** | GPA (core and overall), NCAA Eligibility Center ID (if registered), intended major if known |
| **Stats** | Key stats from circuit and prep play — points, assists, rebounds, shooting percentages. Context matters: "15 PPG in Jr EYBL" carries more weight than "25 PPG in local league." |
| **Video** | Links to highlight reel AND full game film (see Film section below) |
| **Schedule** | Upcoming events where coaches can see you play — especially during evaluation periods |
| **Contact** | Parent email and phone. Your club/prep coach's contact info. |

### How Much Do Recruiting Services Cost?

- **Free profiles** on NCSA and most platforms give you basic visibility
- **Paid tiers** ($500-$3,000+) offer features like coach matching, email outreach tools, and dedicated advisors
- **Are they worth it?** The paid services can help with organization and outreach — but they can't make a coach want a player who isn't good enough. The platform is a tool, not a shortcut. Don't spend money you can't afford on a service that promises outcomes only your kid's talent can deliver.

## Emailing College Coaches

This is the most underutilized recruiting tool — and it's free. Your kid (or you, as a parent) can reach out to college coaches at any time. There are no NCAA restrictions on prospect-initiated contact.

### When to Start

- **Grade 10-11 (Alberta) / US Sophomore-Junior year:** Start sending introductory emails. Coaches can't respond with personalized contact until June 15 after sophomore year (grade 11 in Alberta), but they CAN read your email and watch your film before that.
- **Earlier than grade 10:** Generally too early. Focus on development. Filling out recruiting questionnaires on school websites is fine at any age.

### What Coaches Want to See in an Email

College coaches receive hundreds of recruiting emails. Most get deleted. The ones that get opened and read share these characteristics:

**Subject line matters.** Include your position, height, graduation year, and something specific. Example: *"2032 | 6'3 PG | Jr EYBL West — Genesis Basketball"*

**Keep it short.** 5-8 sentences max. Coaches don't read essays.

**Include these elements:**
1. Brief intro — who you are, where you play, your graduation year (CAN and US class)
2. Why you're interested in THEIR program specifically — show you've done research. Don't send the same generic email to 50 schools.
3. Key stats with context (see stat guidance below)
4. Academic info — GPA, NCAA Eligibility Center ID if registered
5. Links to film — highlight reel AND full game film
6. Your coach's contact info — coaches will often verify interest through your coach
7. **Your upcoming schedule during evaluation periods** — this is critical for Canadian players. A coach who's interested needs to know WHERE they can see you play live. "I'll be at the Dallas EYBL session April 3-5" is what gets you put on a travel list.

### What Stats to Include (by Position)

Don't just say "key stats." Coaches want specific numbers WITH context — the competition level and number of games matter as much as the numbers themselves.

**Guards (PG/SG):**
- PPG, APG, SPG, FG%, 3P%, FT%, turnovers per game
- Example: *"14.2 PPG, 5.1 APG, 2.3 SPG, 42% FG, 36% 3P in 18 games (Jr EYBL West + MADE Hoops)"*

**Wings/Forwards (SF/PF):**
- PPG, RPG, FG%, 3P% (if applicable), blocks/steals per game
- Example: *"16.5 PPG, 8.2 RPG, 1.4 BPG, 48% FG in 15 games (EYCL)"*

**Centers:**
- PPG, RPG, BPG, FG%, FT%
- Example: *"12.0 PPG, 10.1 RPG, 2.8 BPG, 55% FG in 16 games (3SSB)"*

**Always include:** Number of games and the circuit/league. "15 PPG in 12 games at Jr EYBL" means something. "15 PPG" alone means nothing — a coach doesn't know if that's against elite competition or a local rec league.

### Sample Email Template

> Subject: 2032 | 6'3 PG | Jr EYBL West — [Program Name]
>
> Dear Coach [Last Name],
>
> My name is [Name], a [position] from [City, Province, Canada]. I play for [Club/Program] and I'm a Canadian Class of [Year] / US Class of [Year].
>
> I'm reaching out because [specific reason you're interested in their program — mention something real: their playing style, a player you admire on their roster, their academic program, etc.].
>
> This season I averaged [key stats] playing [circuit/league]. My core GPA is [X.X] and I'm registered with the NCAA Eligibility Center (ID: [number]).
>
> Here are links to my film:
> - Highlight reel: [link]
> - Full game vs. [opponent] at [event]: [link]
>
> My upcoming schedule includes [upcoming events during evaluation periods].
>
> My coach, [Coach Name] ([email/phone]), can speak to my abilities and character. I'd welcome any opportunity to learn more about your program.
>
> Thank you for your time,
> [Name]
> [Phone] | [Email]

### What NOT to Include

- **Don't say your kid is "the hardest worker" or "the most coachable player."** Every parent says this. Let the coach form that impression from film and from talking to your coach.
- **Don't include rankings from Instagram accounts** the coach has never heard of.
- **Don't attach video files.** Send YouTube/Hudl links, not 15MB attachments that clog inboxes.
- **Don't write an essay** about your kid's character, journey, or how much they've overcome. Save it for the visit. The email gets them to watch the film — that's its only job.
- **Don't send the same email to 50 schools.** Coaches can tell immediately. Personalize or don't bother.

### Follow-Up

- **If you don't hear back:** A polite follow-up 2-3 weeks later is appropriate, not pushy. Coaches are busy — your email may have been read and flagged for later.
- **If a coach responds and asks for more film, your schedule, or your coach's contact:** Respond within 24 hours. Speed signals genuine interest. Waiting a week tells the coach you're not that serious.
- **If a coach doesn't respond after 2 follow-ups:** Move on to other programs. Silence is an answer.

## Film — What Coaches Actually Want

Film is the #1 recruiting tool for Canadian players. US coaches can easily attend EYBL events near them. They're not flying to Calgary. Your film is how they evaluate your kid.

### Highlight Reel

**Length:** Under 4 minutes. Many coaches watch 20-30 seconds before deciding whether to keep watching. Put your best plays first.

**Content:** 20-30 plays that demonstrate your top 2-3 skills. Organize by skill (shooting, playmaking, defense) rather than mixing randomly.

**What to show:**
- Offense AND defense — a reel with only dunks and crossovers tells coaches nothing about your complete game
- Basketball IQ — reads, passes, off-ball movement, not just individual plays
- Athleticism in game context — not just a workout or open gym
- Competition level matters — plays against Jr EYBL competition carry more weight than plays against a local league

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't open with a logo animation, music intro, or 30 seconds of text. Start with your best play immediately.
- Don't include plays against obviously weak competition
- Don't only show scoring — coaches evaluate the whole player

### Full Game Film

**This is more important than the highlight reel.** A highlight reel gets a coach interested. Full game film is what they evaluate for a scholarship decision.

- Provide 2-3 complete, unedited games against the strongest competition available
- Film from mid-court or elevated angle (not behind the baseline) so coaches can see off-ball movement and defensive positioning
- Make sure your kid is clearly identifiable (jersey number in the file name or description)
- Games from circuit events or prep school leagues carry the most weight

<Callout type="tip" title="Film quality matters less than you think">
Coaches don't need professional production quality. A steady camera from the stands at mid-court is fine. What they need is: the full game, unedited, at a wide enough angle to see the whole court. They're evaluating how your kid plays, not how good your videographer is.
</Callout>

### Where to Host Film

- **YouTube** (unlisted links) — universal, free, easy to share
- **Hudl** — widely used in US recruiting, coaches are familiar with it
- **NCSA profile** — video library built into your recruiting profile
- **Google Drive** — works but less professional than YouTube/Hudl

## The Role of Your Coach

Your club or prep coach's relationships with college coaches are one of the most important recruiting channels — especially for Canadian players.

**What a good coach does for recruiting:**
- Reaches out to college coaches on behalf of their players
- Has existing relationships with college programs from years of placing players
- Advocates for your kid's character and ability — college coaches trust evaluations from coaches they know
- Facilitates communication between your family and college programs

**What to ask your coach:**
- "Which college coaches do you have relationships with?"
- "Have you placed players at the college level before? Where?"
- "Will you reach out to programs on my kid's behalf?"
- "Can I list you as a reference in my recruiting emails?"

**If your coach has no college connections:** Your recruiting path is harder — not impossible, but significantly more self-directed. Consider whether a move to a program with stronger college relationships makes sense. This is one of the most important (and least discussed) factors in choosing a program.

## College Camps — An Underutilized Channel

College basketball camps — run by a school's coaching staff on their campus — are a legitimate and often overlooked recruiting channel, especially for Canadian players who may not have heavy circuit exposure.

**Why camps matter:**
- You're playing directly in front of the coaching staff that would recruit you — for an extended evaluation, not just a passing glance at a circuit event
- Camps give coaches time to evaluate your game, your attitude, your coachability, and how you interact with other players
- It's a way to show genuine interest in a specific program — attending a school's camp signals that you're serious about them

**How to use camps strategically:**
- Identify 5-10 target schools and check whether they offer summer basketball camps
- Many schools offer "elite" or "prospect" camps specifically designed for serious recruits (separate from general skills camps)
- Combine camp attendance with unofficial visits — see the campus, meet the staff, get evaluated, all in one trip
- For Canadian families, this means US travel — but attending 2-3 targeted camps can be a more efficient use of your summer budget than attending circuit events where the coaches you want may or may not be present

**The math:** A camp costs $200-$500 + travel. If it puts your kid directly in front of a coaching staff they're targeting, the ROI per dollar can be higher than a circuit trip where coach attendance is less certain.

## A Typical Recruiting Calendar

Here's a rough annual calendar for what happens when. Exact dates shift by year — always verify with the [official NCAA recruiting calendar](https://www.ncaa.org/sports/2018/5/8/division-i-and-ii-recruiting-calendars.aspx).

| Month | NCAA Period | What's Happening |
|---|---|---|
| **September-October** | Recruiting/Contact | School basketball begins (Canada). Club tryouts. Fill out recruiting questionnaires. Coaches can contact juniors/seniors. |
| **November** | Recruiting + Dead periods | **Early signing period** (mid-Nov, senior year only). School basketball season. Short dead period. |
| **December-February** | Recruiting + Dead periods | School season. Winter training. Club prep. Some winter circuit events. Short dead periods around holidays. |
| **March** | Recruiting | Circuit season begins. Jr EYBL Super Regional (Memphis). |
| **April-May** | **Evaluation** | **Coaches can watch you play.** Circuit regional sessions. NCAA spring evaluation weekends. Jr EYBL Open Events. This is when to be on the court. |
| **June** | Quiet → **Evaluation** | Quiet period (early June — campus visits only). **June 15: coaches can initiate contact with sophomores.** Evaluation period begins mid-June. College camps. |
| **July** | **Evaluation** | **The most important month.** Circuit championships (Peach Jam, 3SSB finals, UAA finals). Maximum coach attendance. Peak evaluation. |
| **August** | Quiet | Campus visits. Prep school decisions. Coaches can only meet on campus. |

**Note:** This calendar is a general framework. Specific dates for evaluation periods, dead periods, and signing periods change annually. The NCAA publishes the official calendar each year at [ncaa.org](https://www.ncaa.org/sports/2018/5/8/division-i-and-ii-recruiting-calendars.aspx).

## Key Takeaways

1. **Your kid can email coaches at any time.** There are no NCAA restrictions on prospect-initiated contact. Start in grade 10-11.
2. **Short, specific, personalized emails with film links.** Generic mass emails get deleted. Show you've researched the program.
3. **Full game film > highlight reel.** Highlights get attention. Game film gets scholarships. Provide both.
4. **Film against strong competition.** A 30-point game at Jr EYBL carries 10x more weight than a 30-point game at a local tournament.
5. **Your coach's relationships matter enormously.** A coach who knows college coaches and advocates for your kid is a massive recruiting advantage.
6. **Recruiting platforms are tools, not shortcuts.** Have profiles on NCSA and Prep Hoops, but don't confuse platform presence with actual recruiting.
7. **Follow up.** Coaches are busy. A polite follow-up email 2-3 weeks after your initial outreach is appropriate.
