# A Parent's Decision Framework

Basketball development isn't one big decision — it's a series of smaller ones, spread across years, each building on the last. The problem is that most families face these decisions without a clear framework for thinking through them. You're making calls based on what other parents are doing, what a coach told you, or what looked good on Instagram — and those aren't great decision-making tools.

This guide walks through the key decision points in order, from choosing a first club through the prep school question. For each one, we lay out what matters, what doesn't, and how to think about it honestly.

**Before we start — a grounding principle:** At every stage, the right question is **"Is my kid developing, enjoying the game, and being challenged appropriately?"** — not "Is my kid getting exposure?" or "Is my kid on the best team?" Development drives everything. Exposure is an outcome of development, not a substitute for it.

### The 5 Decisions at a Glance

| Decision | Key Question | When |
|---|---|---|
| **[Which club?](#decision-1-which-club-should-we-join)** | Is the coaching developing my kid? Will they play meaningful minutes? | Ages 10-13 (revisit annually) |
| **[US travel?](#decision-2-how-early-should-we-consider-traveling-to-the-us-to-play)** | What will my kid get from this trip that they can't get at home? | Ages 13-14 (Jr circuits), 15+ (HS) |
| **[Which circuits?](#decision-3-what-circuits-should-we-play-on)** | What level is my kid at? Does our program have circuit access? | Ages 13-17 (driven by program) |
| **[Prep school?](#decision-4-should-we-consider-the-prep-school-route)** | Does my kid need more development, exposure, or academic prep? | Start at grade 10-11. Decide by 12. |
| **[Prep + circuits?](#decision-5-do-we-need-to-play-both-prep-and-circuits)** | Which stage is my kid at? What does each provide? | Ongoing |

## First: Where Does My Kid Fit?

Before making any decisions about clubs, circuits, or prep schools, you need an honest assessment of where your kid is — and what's realistic.

### The numbers most parents don't want to hear

Every parent thinks their kid is exceptional. That's natural — and it's also the single biggest source of poor decision-making in youth basketball.

Here are the numbers (US data — [source: NCAA](https://www.ncaa.org/sports/2015/3/6/men-s-basketball-probability-of-competing-beyond-high-school.aspx)):

| Stat | Number |
|---|---|
| High school boys basketball players | ~540,000 |
| Play NCAA basketball (D1, D2, D3 combined) | ~18,800 (**3.5%**) |
| Play NCAA Division 1 | ~5,400 (**1.0%**) |
| Odds of a HS player making a D1 roster | **1 in 105** |
| Odds of making any college roster | **1 in 18** |
| D1 scholarships available per team | **13 max** |
| D1 athletes receiving some form of athletic aid | **~51%** |

**Important context:** These stats include every kid who plays casual high school basketball. If your kid is already competing on elite circuits, training seriously, and in the top tier of their age group, their odds are meaningfully better than 1 in 105. But the general numbers matter for perspective — they should inform how much financial risk you take, not whether your kid should pursue the game.

This doesn't mean your kid can't make it. It means decisions should be grounded in honest assessment, not fantasy.

**The healthy mindset:** Pursue the competitive pathway because your kid loves basketball, is developing as a player and person, and the investment is sustainable for your family. If post-secondary basketball happens, that's an incredible bonus. If it doesn't, your kid has had years of competition, teamwork, discipline, and growth that serve them for life.

### Be honest about level

| Level | What It Looks Like | Realistic Path |
|---|---|---|
| **Elite (top 1-5% nationally)** | Dominant against top competition. Circuit programs are recruiting your kid. Provincial/national team consideration. | NCAA D1 is realistic. Circuit → prep → D1 is the typical path. |
| **Very competitive (top 5-15%)** | Strong against good competition. On a circuit team or sought by one. Developing rapidly. | D1 is possible with continued development. D2, USports, or low-major D1 are strong options. Prep year is important. |
| **Competitive (top 15-30%)** | Solid player. Holds their own against good competition but not dominant. | USports, D2, D3, NAIA are realistic targets. Prep year could help with development and exposure. |
| **Developing** | Shows potential but needs significant development. Competes locally. | Focus on development and enjoyment. The pathway is long — if the talent emerges, doors will open. Don't force it. |

### The questions that matter

1. **Does my kid love the game?** Not "do they like winning" or "do I want this for them" — do THEY love playing basketball? The grind of competitive basketball only works if the player is internally motivated. If the love isn't there, no amount of money, training, or circuit play will create it.
2. **Are they a good teammate?** This matters more than most parents realize. College coaches recruit players who make teams better — players who defend, pass, communicate, and compete. It's extremely common for multiple players from the same team to get recruited. The kid who makes everyone around them better is more recruitable than the kid who scores 30 and loses.
3. **Are they improving?** Not "are they the best on their team" — are they getting better? Development trajectory matters more than current ranking. A player who's improving rapidly at 15 is more exciting to coaches than a player who peaked at 13.
4. **Can our family sustain the investment?** The financial and time commitment escalates at each stage. Be honest about what you can afford without straining the family. Remember: only ~3-4% of HS players compete at any college level. Make financial decisions accordingly.
5. **What's the plan if basketball doesn't work out?** The vast majority of competitive players do not play college basketball. Is your kid getting an education, building character, and developing as a person — regardless of basketball outcomes? The best basketball families plan for success AND have a strong backup plan.

## Decision 1: Which Club Should We Join?

*Find quality coaching and a program where your kid actually plays — prestige is overrated.*

This is the first real decision most families face, and it sets the trajectory for everything that follows. The club your kid joins determines the coaching they receive, the competition they face, the circuits they access, and the culture they develop in.

### What matters most

**Coaching quality** — above everything else. A great coach at a modest club will develop your kid better than a mediocre coach at a prestigious one. Look for coaches who:
- Teach the game (reads, decision-making, fundamentals) rather than just running plays
- Develop all players on the roster, not just the top 2-3
- Prioritize long-term development over short-term winning
- Communicate clearly with parents and treat players with respect
- Have a track record — where have their past players gone?

**Development culture** — Is the program focused on making players better, or on winning trophies? At the youth level (under 15), development should come first. Programs that optimize for winning at 12-13 often do so at the expense of individual player growth (shorter rotations, system-dependent play, limiting players to one position).

**Circuit access and pathway** — Does this club have a path to meaningful competition? This doesn't mean they need to be on EYBL right now — but is there a trajectory? A club that plays local tournaments only and has no ambition to compete at higher levels will eventually limit a serious player's development.

**Playing time — the non-negotiable**

This deserves its own callout because it's the single most common mistake parents make when choosing a club or travel team:

**Your kid needs to go where they're actually going to play.**

This assumes the coaching is strong and development is a priority (especially at younger ages) — because playing 30 minutes per game under a bad coach isn't the answer either. But given quality coaching, playing time is where development happens. Not on the bench. Not watching. Playing.

- A slightly lower-level team where your kid plays 25+ minutes, handles the ball, makes decisions, and is challenged is **almost always better** than a top team where they play 8 minutes and watch the stars.
- Players develop through reps — game reps, decision-making reps, failure-and-recovery reps. You can't get those from the bench.
- Parents are often drawn to the "best" team for the prestige. But prestige doesn't develop players. Minutes do.
- Ask the program honestly: how many players get meaningful minutes? What does the rotation typically look like? If a team carries 15 players and plays 8, do the math on where your kid falls.

**The formula:** Quality coaching + meaningful playing time + appropriate level of competition = development. Remove any one of those three and you're wasting time and money.

### What matters less than you think

- **The club's brand or reputation.** A well-known name doesn't guarantee good coaching or development for YOUR kid. The "best" team in your city might not be the best team for your kid.
- **What other parents are doing.** The fact that "everyone" is going to a certain club doesn't mean it's right for your family.
- **Travel team status at age 10-11.** At young ages, the priority should be playing lots of basketball, having fun, and building fundamental skills — not being on the most competitive travel team.

### The honest question to ask yourself

**Where is your kid actually at — right now, not where you hope they'll be?** A club that's too high means bench time and frustration. A club that's too low means they're not challenged. Find the level where they'll play, be challenged, and develop under quality coaching. And be honest about it.


## Decision 2: How Early Should We Consider Traveling to the US to Play?

*Match the investment to the stage. Development trips at 13-14; exposure trips at 15+.*

This is where things start getting expensive — and where the development vs. exposure question gets real.

### The general timeline

**Under 13 (before 7th grade):** For most families, US travel is not necessary. Focus on local and regional competition, skill development, and finding the right club. The rare exception is a truly elite player who's already been identified by a circuit program — but even then, the basketball benefit at this age is development, not exposure.

**13-14 (7th-8th grade / Jr circuits):** This is when US travel enters the picture for competitive players. Jr EYBL, Jr 3SSB, MADE Hoops, and other middle school circuits are US-based. If your kid is on a program that competes on these circuits, US travel becomes part of the deal.

**15+ (HS circuits):** If your kid is pursuing post-secondary basketball, US circuit play during NCAA evaluation periods is where real exposure happens. This is when the investment in travel starts to have recruiting ROI.

### How to think about it

**The question isn't "should we travel to the US?" — it's "what will my kid get from this specific trip that they can't get at home?"**

- **Development benefit:** Will they face competition that stretches them? At 13-14, playing against top US talent in a Jr EYBL session is a genuine development experience — even if no college coach is watching.
- **Exposure benefit:** At 15+, is this event during an NCAA evaluation period? Will college coaches actually be in the building? If the answer is no, the exposure argument falls apart.
- **Financial reality:** Each US trip costs $1,500-$3,000+ (flights, hotels, meals, transport). Three to five trips per season adds up fast. Can your family sustain this without financial strain? If it's straining the budget, it's not the right time.

### What to watch out for

- **Traveling to the US for the sake of "being there."** If the event isn't during an evaluation period and the competition isn't meaningfully higher than what's available at home, the trip is a vacation with basketball, not a development investment.
- **Keeping up with other families.** Some families can afford 5 US trips per season. If you can't, that doesn't mean your kid is falling behind. It means you need to be strategic about which trips matter most.
- **Burnout at young ages.** Year-round US travel at 12-13 can burn kids out before they reach the ages where it matters most (15-18). Protect your kid's love of the game.

### The bottom line

Match the investment to the stage. At 13-14, US travel is about development and acclimation — budget accordingly and don't overdo it. At 15+, US travel during evaluation periods is about exposure and has real recruiting value — invest strategically.


## Decision 3: What Circuits Should We Play On?

*Don't chase circuits your kid isn't ready for. This is really a program selection question.*

Once your kid is at a level where circuit play makes sense, the question becomes which circuits to pursue. This decision is often made by your program director (not by individual families), but understanding it helps you choose the right program and set expectations.

### The key factors

**What level is your kid / team at?**
- **Elite (top 5% nationally):** EYBL, 3SSB, UAA — the flagship circuits. Your program needs Nike, Adidas, or UA partnership to access these.
- **Very competitive (top 10-15%):** EYCL, 3SGB, UA Rise, MADE Hoops Circuit — strong second-tier circuits with real coaching attention.
- **Competitive (top 20-25%):** Jr EYBL Open Events, MADE Hoops events, Prep Hoops events, Uncle Drew Circuit, Puma NXT — legitimate competition and some exposure, more accessible.
- **Developing:** Local and regional tournaments, provincial competitions — focus on development and building a competitive foundation before chasing circuits.

**Evaluation period alignment:** Does this circuit schedule events during NCAA evaluation periods (primarily June-July)? If not, the exposure value is limited. (See [Recruiting Reality](/circuits/recruiting-reality) for the full evaluation period breakdown.)

**Geographic accessibility:** Some circuits have western US stops that are more practical from Alberta/Western Canada. Others are concentrated in the East or Midwest. Factor travel logistics into the decision.

**Your program's circuit access:** Individual families don't typically choose circuits — programs do. The circuit question is really a program selection question. If you want your kid on EYBL, you need to be on an EYBL program. (See [Evaluating Programs](/circuits/evaluating-programs) for how to assess this.)

### What NOT to do

- **Don't chase circuits that your kid isn't ready for.** Playing on a circuit where your kid is overmatched is worse than playing at the right level. Development happens when players are challenged but competitive — not when they're getting blown out every game.
- **Don't confuse "being on a circuit" with development.** The circuit provides the platform. Development comes from coaching, practice, and the player's own work. A kid on EYBL who doesn't practice outside of circuit events isn't developing faster than a kid at a local club who trains every day.
- **Don't spread too thin.** Some families try to play on multiple circuits simultaneously. More events ≠ more development. Quality of competition and coaching matter more than volume of games.


## Decision 4: Should We Consider the Prep School Route?

*Start this conversation in grade 10-11 — not when your kid is about to graduate.*

This is one of the biggest decisions in the Canadian basketball pathway — both financially and developmentally. (See the full [Prep Schools guide](/schools/prep-schools) for detailed evaluation criteria.)

### When to start thinking about it

**Grade 10-11 is when this conversation should begin** — not when your kid is about to graduate. Prep school applications, program evaluation, and financial planning take time. If you wait until grade 12, your options narrow.

### The decision framework

Ask these five questions:

**1. Does my kid need more development time before college?**
- If yes (physically, skill-wise, or mentally not ready for college basketball) → prep year is worth considering
- If no (already has the body, skills, and maturity to compete at the college level) → may not need prep

**2. Does my kid need more exposure?**
- If they've been on elite circuits (EYBL, 3SSB, UAA) with heavy evaluation-period visibility and have genuine college interest → they may be able to skip prep
- If they haven't had significant circuit exposure → prep school provides a concentrated year of visibility
- **But:** not all prep schools provide equal exposure. A program that doesn't compete during evaluation periods or in recognized leagues may not solve this problem. (See [Prep Schools](/schools/prep-schools) for vetting criteria.)

**3. Are there academic gaps?**
- If your kid's courses don't fully align with NCAA Eligibility Center requirements → a prep year with accredited academics can fill the gaps
- If academics are solid and Eligibility Center requirements are met → less urgency on this front

**4. Can we afford it?**
- Prep school tuition: $10,000-$40,000+ per year
- Room and board if relocating (many top programs are in Ontario)
- Travel to US events
- Be honest about what your family can sustain without financial strain. A prep year should be an investment with a clear return, not a financial Hail Mary.

**5. Is this the right program?**
- Even if the answer to questions 1-4 is "yes, prep makes sense," the wrong program can waste a year and tens of thousands of dollars. The program evaluation matters as much as the decision to go prep. (See the [vetting checklist](/schools/prep-schools).)

### The option to go to the US

Some Canadian families send their kid to a US prep school or even a US high school instead of staying in Canada. This can offer higher competition and more direct visibility — but comes with higher costs, visa requirements, and distance from home. It's the right move for some families, but it's not the only path.


## Decision 5: Do We Need to Play Both Prep and Circuits?

*They're not either/or — circuits during high school, prep after. Most Canadian players do both.*

### The typical sequence

1. **Club basketball + circuits (ages 13-17):** This is where development and early exposure happen. Club training provides the daily work. Circuit play provides elite competition during evaluation periods.

2. **Prep school (post-grade 12, sometimes earlier):** This is where concentrated development + exposure + academics come together in a structured environment. Prep school league play (NPA, etc.) provides competition. Many prep schools also compete in US tournaments during evaluation periods.

### Can you do both at the same time?

**During high school (15-17):** Your kid may play for their prep school team during the school year AND play on a circuit team during the spring/summer evaluation periods. This is common and can work well — the school provides the daily development, and the circuit provides the summer exposure.

**During the post-grad prep year:** The prep school's own league and tournament schedule typically IS the competition vehicle. Some players also play on a circuit team in the summer, but this depends on the program's schedule and policies.

### The circuit-only path

Some players skip prep entirely and go directly from high school to college. This works when:
- They've been on elite circuits with heavy evaluation-period exposure
- They have genuine, committable offers in hand
- They're physically, mentally, and academically ready for college

This path provides potentially less overall exposure and development time than the prep route — but it's a legitimate option for the right player.

### The prep-only path

Some players — especially those who didn't play on major circuits during high school — use the prep year as their primary competitive platform. The prep school's league play, US travel, and coaching connections become the recruiting pathway.

This can work if the prep program has strong league affiliation (NPA or equivalent) and competes during evaluation periods. If the prep program is isolated and doesn't play in front of coaches — circuits would have been a better investment.

### The honest answer

**For most Canadian players pursuing NCAA basketball, the path includes both circuits and prep — just at different stages.** Circuits during high school, prep year after. The question isn't "which one?" — it's "which circuits at what age, and which prep program at the end?"


## A Note on Every Decision

Every decision in this framework comes back to the same core question:

**Is this the right move for THIS kid, at THIS stage, given THIS family's circumstances?**

Not what other families are doing. Not what Instagram says. Not what a program director is selling you. What's right for your kid — their talent level, their maturity, their goals, and your family's financial reality.

The best players in Canada didn't all follow the same path. Some played EYBL from 13. Some didn't play circuits until 16. Some did two prep years. Some skipped prep entirely. The common thread isn't the path — it's that at every stage, someone made honest assessments and smart investments.

That's what this framework is for.
